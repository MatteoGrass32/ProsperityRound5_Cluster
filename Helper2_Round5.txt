It’s a Lot
SELECTED
Orin
Fifty tradable goods. I remember the first time I sat down in front of a data set that size. It was around that time that silly little tech company just pulled the most absurd IPO. What was its name again?... Oh. You couldn’t be bothered with that kind of trivia, could you now, dear? I just remember having no system whatsoever. It took me three days to realize I was looking at it completely wrong.  The mistake most people make with large data sets is trying to read everything at once. Fifty products. Fifty stories. Fifty sets of numbers moving in fifty different directions. That is not analysis. Oh no. Then it all looks like noise, dear. And noise has a way of convincing you that you are making progress when you are simply doing busy work.  The answer, as I eventually worked out, is grouping. Can you cluster these products into categories that share structural characteristics? Do certain groups consistently outperform others? Do some segments reliably fall short, round after round, regardless of conditions? Patterns at the cluster level are far more readable than patterns at the individual product level, and far more actionable too.  Opportunity in a large data set rarely announces itself. It tends to sit quietly inside a pattern, waiting for someone patient enough to look at the right level of abstraction. Start with the clusters, love. The individual products will start making a lot more sense once you do.
Pairing for Profit
SELECTED
Orin
Finding a profitable category is satisfying. I will not pretend otherwise. But satisfaction has a way of stopping people exactly where the real work begins. The more important question is never what performed well. It is why.  Do the products within your strongest clusters share measurable similarities? Is there something structural that explains why they move together, or why one tends to follow another with a slight delay? Markets are full of relationships like that, if you know how to look for them. Some products are practically old friends. Did I tell you about my old friend Diego? Diego knows how to spot a cluster. That man has laser eyes! Or he used to anyway. I guess he has lazy eyes now. Aging was not all too easy on his eyesight, I am afraid. Still, you should definitely meet him. Oh, what am I babbling about. We were talking about product relationships. They move together, diverge under specific conditions, and then quietly reconcile again once those conditions pass.  Ask yourself whether the relationships you observe are stable or whether they shift over time. A stable relationship can confirm a signal you were already watching. A shifting one can tell you something has fundamentally changed. Both are useful. Neither should be ignored.  Understanding products in isolation is a start. Understanding how they relate to one another is where conviction comes from.
Same but Slower
SELECTED
Orin
Timing, my dear. It is always about timing. Not in the dramatic sense that people mean when they say it. Not about being first or fastest. About understanding that similar things do not always happen at the same moment, and that the gap between them is often more useful than the things themselves.  Some products react quickly. Others take their time. That delay is not a flaw in the market. It is a feature, if you know how to read it. I have watched this play out across more decades than I care to count. One category moves. Another follows. The gap between them repeats, not perfectly, but consistently enough to be meaningful.  So ask yourself whether you can measure that lag. Is there evidence of a recurring timing gap between related goods? Does one category consistently lead while another consistently follows? If the answer is yes, and if that gap is stable enough to be relied upon, then you are no longer reacting to the market. You are anticipating it. Which is, I will tell you now, a considerably more comfortable place to trade from.  Recognizing structural similarity is one step, love. Recognizing sequence is another thing entirely. One tells you what is related. The other tells you what is coming.
